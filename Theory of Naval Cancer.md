### Epidemiological Basis: Literal Naval Cancer Risks
Scientific evidence substantiates elevated cancer risks in naval contexts:

- **Asbestos Exposure on Ships**: From the 1930s to 1980s, over 3,300 U.S. Navy ships used asbestos for insulation and fireproofing, exposing sailors to fibers linked to mesothelioma, lung cancer, and asbestosis. A 2021 review found sailors at higher risk due to confined living/working spaces, with studies reporting excess morbidity from these diseases.

- **Solvent and Contaminant Exposures**: At bases like Camp Lejeune, volatile organic compounds (e.g., trichloroethylene, benzene) in drinking water increased risks of myeloid cancers, leukemias, and lymphomas. A 2024 cohort study showed adjusted hazard ratios (aHRs) >1.2 for acute myeloid leukemia and other myeloid neoplasms among exposed Marines/Navy personnel compared to unexposed controls.

- **Radiation from Nuclear Ships**: Personnel on nuclear-powered vessels face 9-fold expected cancer rates from low-level ionizing radiation, per a 2020 analysis of U.S. Navy data. Additional risks include radionuclides at shipyards, linked to leukemias and bone cancers.

These risks persist post-service, with veterans comprising ~33% of mesothelioma cases.
